Key Vocabulary — 5 Words This Unit
algorithm — a step-by-step set of instructions that a computer (or person) follows to complete a task
argument — a claim supported by evidence and reasoning, designed to persuade
counterclaim — a position that challenges or opposes your main argument
bias — a tendency to favor one perspective or outcome, which can distort reasoning or evidence
ethics — the study of right and wrong, especially in situations where values or interests conflict
W1Reading About Technology
Mon What is code? History of computing + who Turing was Reading ▾
  1. Open your computing history article (mom will have it ready).
  2. Read it all the way through once without stopping.
  3. Go back and circle or highlight any part about Alan Turing.
  4. Tell mom one thing Turing did that surprised you.
💬 Say out loud: "The thing I found most interesting was…" or "I didn't know that computers were invented because…"
✓ Done when: you've read the article and shared one Turing fact out loud.
Tue Read "What is AI?" article — find the main claims Reading ▾
  1. Read the "What is AI and how does it work?" article.
  2. While reading, put a star next to any sentence that makes a big claim (like "AI will change everything").
  3. Find two sentences that are claims — things the writer is arguing, not just facts.
  4. Tell mom the two claims you found.
💬 Say out loud: "The writer claims that…" or "One thing they're arguing is…"
✓ Done when: you've found and explained two claims from the article.
Wed Write: explain how a Scratch program works to someone who's never seen code Writing ▾
  1. Think of something from real life that works like code — a recipe, a traffic light, or a vending machine.
  2. Open Google Docs and write your analogy at the top: "A Scratch program is like a [your choice] because…"
  3. Explain what "input" means using your analogy (e.g., pressing buttons on a vending machine).
  4. Explain what "process" means (e.g., the machine deciding what to give you).
  5. Explain what "output" means (e.g., the snack drops out).
  6. Write 3–5 sentences total. Short is fine — clarity wins.
💬 If writing feels hard, say it out loud first: "OK so a Scratch program is kind of like a [thing]…" — then type what you said.
✓ Done when: you have 3–5 sentences in Google Docs that explain input, process, and output using a real-life analogy.
Thu Open Scratch — build a simple animation or interactive story Computer ▾
  1. Open Scratch (scratch.mit.edu) — log in or use the offline editor.
  2. Choose a sprite (character) and a background.
  3. Add at least one "when green flag clicked" block to start your animation.
  4. Make something happen — the sprite moves, says something, or changes costume.
  5. Press the green flag and watch it work.
  6. If you want to keep going and add more, great — but one working thing is the goal.
💬 If you get stuck, just try a different block and see what it does — experimenting is the right move in coding.
✓ Done when: you have a Scratch project where something happens when you press the green flag.
Fri Demo your Scratch animation — explain what each block does Discussion ▾
  1. Open your Scratch project from yesterday.
  2. Show mom (or whoever is around) the finished animation.
  3. Point to each block in the code and say what it does in plain English.
  4. If you don't know what a block does, try removing it and see what breaks — that's how you find out.
💬 Say: "This block makes it… and this one tells it to… and if I take this one out, then…"
✓ Done when: you've explained at least three blocks in your Scratch project out loud.
Weekly Writing Prompt

Explain how a computer program works to someone who has never seen one. Use a real-life analogy (recipe, traffic light, vending machine) to explain input, process, and output.

"A Scratch program is kind of like a ________, because…"

Length: 3–5 sentences. One clear idea is better than a long confusing explanation.

✅ Weekly Checklist
W2Code Documentation
Mon What is a README? Read examples from real projects Reading ▾
  1. Mom will pull up 2–3 README examples from real open-source coding projects.
  2. Read through them and notice: what does each one tell you?
  3. Find the part that explains what the project does.
  4. Find the part that explains how to use it.
  5. Tell mom: if you had no idea what this project was, would this README help you? Why or why not?
💬 Say: "A good README is like a… because it tells you…"
✓ Done when: you've read two READMEs and shared your opinion on whether they were helpful.
Tue Writing workshop: who is the audience? What do they need to know? Writing ▾
  1. Think about a piece of tech you know really well — your phone, a game, a device.
  2. Imagine explaining it to your grandparent who has never used it.
  3. What are the three most important things they'd need to know?
  4. Write those three things as bullet points in Google Docs.
  5. Then answer: what words would you NOT use when explaining to them?
💬 "If I had to teach my grandparent to use [thing], I'd start by explaining… and I'd avoid saying…"
✓ Done when: you have 3 bullet points explaining the tech to a non-expert audience.
Wed Plan your Scratch project: write a design spec Writing ▾
  1. Decide what your main Scratch project will be — a game, a story, a quiz, or something else.
  2. Open Google Docs and write a title for your project.
  3. Write one sentence: "The goal of this project is to…"
  4. Write who it's for: "This project is for people who…"
  5. List three features: "Feature 1: …, Feature 2: …, Feature 3: …"
  6. Write one sentence about how someone plays or uses it.
💬 Say the idea out loud first: "OK so my project is going to be a [thing] where you [do something]…" — then write what you just said.
✓ Done when: your design spec has a title, goal, audience, three features, and how-to-use in Google Docs.
Thu Start building your Scratch project; add comments inside the code Computer ▾
  1. Open Scratch and start a new project.
  2. Build the first part of your project — the opening screen or the first action.
  3. Right-click on one of your blocks and choose "Add Comment."
  4. Write a short note explaining what that section of code does.
  5. Add at least one more comment somewhere else in the code.
💬 Comments are notes to your future self — write them the way you'd explain it to a friend: "This part makes the character jump when you press space."
✓ Done when: you've built the first section of your project and added at least two comments in the code.
Fri Explain your project using only your written spec — no showing the screen Discussion ▾
  1. Close your laptop or turn away from the screen.
  2. Read only from your written design spec document.
  3. Explain your project to mom using only what you wrote — no pointing at the screen.
  4. Ask: did she understand what it does from just the description?
  5. Add one thing to the spec that you realize is missing.
💬 "Based on what I wrote, can you picture what this does? What part is confusing?"
✓ Done when: you've explained the project from the spec alone and updated it with at least one improvement.
Weekly Writing Prompt

Write a design spec for your Scratch project. Include: project title, goal (one sentence), target audience, three main features, and how a user will interact with it.

"My project is called ______. The goal is to… It's designed for… The three main things it does are…"

Length: about 6–8 sentences total. One clear sentence per section.

✅ Weekly Checklist
W3Claude as a Writing Tool
Mon Discuss: how does AI help writing — but not replace it? Reading ▾
  1. Ask mom: "What's the difference between AI helping you write and AI writing for you?"
  2. Talk through it together — there's no wrong answer yet, just thinking out loud.
  3. Think about a time you used a tool that made something easier but you still had to make decisions — cooking, gaming, building something.
  4. Is AI like that? Why or why not? Talk it through.
💬 "I think AI is like [tool] because… but the difference is…"
✓ Done when: you've had a real back-and-forth conversation about what AI does and doesn't do in writing.
Tue Use Claude to help draft or revise a piece of writing — then reflect on it Computer ▾
  1. Open Claude (or Cowork) on the computer.
  2. Paste in a piece of writing you've done recently (the analogy from Week 1, or the design spec).
  3. Ask Claude: "Can you improve this but keep it in my voice?"
  4. Read what it gives back. Notice what changed.
  5. Pick one sentence Claude wrote that you like better than yours — and one that you think misses the point.
  6. Copy both into a new Google Doc with your notes on each.
💬 "This sentence Claude wrote is better because… but this one isn't right because…"
✓ Done when: you have notes on one thing Claude improved and one thing that wasn't right.
Wed Write a reflection on using Claude as a writing tool Writing ▾
  1. Open the Google Doc from yesterday with your notes on Claude's edits.
  2. Write 3–4 sentences answering: what did you ask Claude to do?
  3. Write 1–2 sentences: what did it do well?
  4. Write 1–2 sentences: what was wrong, unhelpful, or just not you?
  5. Quote the one sentence Claude wrote that you kept — put it in quotation marks.
  6. Write one sentence explaining why you kept it or changed it.
💬 Say it out loud first: "I asked Claude to… It was helpful when… But it messed up when…" — then write what you said.
✓ Done when: your reflection has a direct quote from Claude and your opinion on whether it worked.
Thu Continue Scratch project; use Claude to debug or explain an error Computer ▾
  1. Open your Scratch project and keep building.
  2. If something isn't working right, take a screenshot or describe the problem in words.
  3. Go to Claude and ask: "My Scratch project is supposed to [do X] but instead it [does Y]. What might be wrong?"
  4. Read Claude's suggestion and try it in Scratch.
  5. Did it work? Tell mom what Claude suggested and whether it helped.
💬 Claude is great for debugging — describe exactly what you see and what you want, and it will help you figure out the gap.
✓ Done when: you've made progress on the Scratch project and tried using Claude to solve at least one problem.
Fri Share: one thing Claude helped with, one thing you had to do yourself Discussion ▾
  1. Look back at everything you did with Claude this week.
  2. Name one specific thing Claude made easier or better.
  3. Name one specific thing you had to decide yourself — something Claude couldn't do for you.
  4. Share both with mom and talk about why the second one had to be yours.
💬 "Claude helped me with… but I had to decide [thing] myself because… and AI can't do that because…"
✓ Done when: you've named and explained one AI win and one thing that required your own judgment.
Weekly Writing Prompt

Write a reflection on using AI as a writing tool. What did you ask Claude to do? What did it do well? What was wrong or just not you? Quote one sentence Claude wrote and explain what you did with it.

"I asked Claude to… The part that worked was… The part that felt off was… Here's what it wrote: '[quote]' — I [kept/changed/rejected] this because…"

Length: 4–6 sentences. Must include one direct quote from Claude.

✅ Weekly Checklist
W4AI Ethics — Entering the Argument
Mon Read two short op-eds on AI — one pro, one skeptical Reading ▾
  1. Mom will have two short articles ready — one that argues AI is great, one that argues it's risky.
  2. Read the first article and note: what is the writer's main claim?
  3. Read the second article and note: what is their main claim?
  4. For each article, find one piece of evidence they use to back up their claim.
  5. Tell mom which argument you find more convincing — and why.
💬 "This writer argues that… and backs it up with… I found this [more/less] convincing because…"
✓ Done when: you've identified the main claim and one piece of evidence in each article.
Tue Argument structure: claim, reason, evidence, rebuttal — map both articles Writing ▾
  1. Take out yesterday's two articles.
  2. For Article 1, write: CLAIM = [one sentence]. REASON = [why they think that]. EVIDENCE = [what they cite].
  3. For Article 2, do the same thing.
  4. Now write one "rebuttal" — pick one claim and write one sentence pushing back on it.
💬 Say the rebuttal out loud first: "OK they say [X], but the problem with that is…" — then write it.
✓ Done when: both articles are mapped with claim, reason, evidence — plus one written rebuttal.
Wed Choose your position on an AI question — write a claim + 3 reasons outline Writing ▾
  1. Choose one of these questions: (A) Should AI be allowed to grade student writing? (B) Should there be an age limit on AI tools? (C) Does AI make creative work better or worse?
  2. Decide your position — yes, no, or a nuanced answer.
  3. Write your claim in one sentence: "I believe that [position] because…"
  4. Write three separate reasons why — one sentence each.
  5. You'll use these as the foundation for your essay next week.
💬 "I chose [question] because I actually care about it. My position is… and I think that because 1)… 2)… 3)…"
✓ Done when: you have a one-sentence claim and three reasons written in Google Docs.
Thu Research your position — find 2 sources that support it Computer ▾
  1. Open Google and search for your AI question — try phrases like "should AI grade writing pros and cons" or whatever fits your topic.
  2. Find one article or source that supports your position.
  3. Find one more source — doesn't have to be a perfect match, just relevant.
  4. Copy the title and link of each source into your Google Doc.
  5. Write one sentence under each link: what does this source say that helps your argument?
💬 You don't need to read the whole article — skim for one useful fact or claim and write it down.
✓ Done when: two source links are in your Google Doc, each with a one-sentence note on how they help your argument.
Fri Peer debate: defend your position against mom's counter-arguments Discussion ▾
  1. Tell mom your AI question and your position.
  2. Mom will argue the other side — her job is to challenge you.
  3. Your job is to keep defending your position using your three reasons and your sources.
  4. After the debate, tell mom: which of her counter-arguments was hardest to answer?
  5. Write that counter-argument down — you'll need it for your essay.
💬 "My position is [X] because [reason 1]. Even though some people say [counter], I think [response]…"
✓ Done when: you've debated for at least 5 minutes and written down the hardest counter-argument you faced.
Weekly Writing Prompt

Write your AI argument setup: your claim (1 sentence), your three reasons (1 sentence each), and the strongest counter-argument you can imagine (1 sentence).

"I believe that [position]. First, … Second, … Third, … The strongest argument against my view is… but I think…"

Length: 5–7 sentences. This is the skeleton of your essay.

W5Writing the Persuasive Essay
Mon Review essay structure — hook, background, thesis, body, counter, conclusion Writing ▾
  1. Look at the essay structure diagram mom will draw or show you.
  2. Match each part to your own argument: what will your hook be? What's your thesis?
  3. For each of your three reasons, decide what evidence you'll use (from your Week 4 sources).
  4. Identify which counter-argument you'll address (the one from Friday's debate).
  5. Write a brief outline — one bullet per section, just the main idea.
💬 Walk mom through your outline out loud before you write anything: "My intro will… then paragraph 1 is about… paragraph 2 is…"
✓ Done when: you have a bullet-point outline for all five parts of your essay.
Tue Draft your introduction and thesis sentence Writing ▾
  1. Open Google Docs and start a new document titled "[Your AI question] — Essay Draft."
  2. Write a hook — one sentence that starts with something interesting, surprising, or direct.
  3. Write 1–2 sentences of background: what is the issue and why does it matter?
  4. Write your thesis sentence — this should match your claim from Week 4.
  5. Read the intro out loud. Does it sound like you? Is the thesis clear?
💬 Say your hook out loud first: "What if [interesting thing]?" or "Every day, [fact]…" — then write it.
✓ Done when: you have an intro paragraph with a hook, 1–2 background sentences, and a thesis.
Wed Draft three body paragraphs — one reason + one piece of evidence each Writing ▾
  1. For body paragraph 1: write a topic sentence stating your first reason.
  2. Write one sentence of evidence (from your sources or from logic/examples).
  3. Write one sentence explaining how that evidence supports your reason.
  4. Repeat for paragraphs 2 and 3.
  5. Each paragraph: topic sentence + evidence + explanation = 3 sentences minimum.
💬 For each paragraph, say it out loud: "My second reason is… I know this because… This matters because…" — then write it.
✓ Done when: three body paragraphs are drafted, each with a reason, evidence, and explanation.
Thu Counterclaim practice — write the strongest objection to your own argument, then rebut it Writing ▾
  1. Open your essay draft. Read your claim (thesis) out loud.
  2. Now argue against yourself. Write the strongest possible objection — the reason someone who disagrees with you would give. Start with: "One might argue that…"
  3. Write a 2–3 sentence rebuttal. Don't just say "but I'm right." Acknowledge the objection, then explain why your evidence still holds. Start with: "While it is true that… the evidence shows…"
  4. Add your counterclaim + rebuttal paragraph to your essay draft, before your conclusion.
  5. Read the whole essay aloud now. Does it feel more convincing with the counterargument included?
💬 Talk option: Argue both sides out loud first — say the objection as strongly as you can, then rebut it. Then write from what you said.
✓ Done when: counterclaim paragraph (objection + 2–3 sentence rebuttal) added to essay draft
Thu Draft counter-argument paragraph and rebuttal; type full draft Computer ▾
  1. Use the hardest counter-argument from Friday's debate.
  2. Write one sentence fairly stating the other side: "Some people argue that…"
  3. Write your rebuttal: "However, [your response]…"
  4. Make sure your whole essay is in one Google Doc now — intro, 3 body paragraphs, counter+rebuttal.
  5. You still need a conclusion — that comes tomorrow.
💬 "The strongest counter to my argument is… but I disagree because…"
✓ Done when: counter-argument paragraph is drafted and your full draft (minus conclusion) is in one Google Doc.
Fri Peer review: does the argument flow? Is the evidence convincing? Discussion ▾
  1. Read your draft out loud to mom (or have her read it).
  2. Ask her: does the argument make sense in order? Does each paragraph connect to the next?
  3. Ask: is there any point where the evidence feels weak or doesn't really prove the reason?
  4. Write down two specific things you want to improve before the final version.
💬 "Does my argument flow, or is there a jump somewhere? Which part did you find least convincing?"
✓ Done when: you've had peer feedback and written down two specific revision goals.
Weekly Writing Prompt

Write your counter-argument paragraph: state the strongest opposing view fairly (1–2 sentences), then write your rebuttal explaining why your position is still correct (1–2 sentences).

"Some people argue that… They believe this because… However, I think… because…"

Length: 3–4 sentences for the full counter/rebuttal paragraph.

W6Final Project & Showcase
Mon Finalize Scratch project; finish documentation and README Computer ▾
  1. Open your Scratch project and do a final play-through.
  2. Fix any obvious bugs — things that don't work the way you intended.
  3. Check that all your code comments are there and make sense.
  4. Open your design spec document from Week 2.
  5. Update it to match the final project — did anything change from the original plan? Note it.
💬 A good README honestly says if the plan changed and why — that's real documentation, not cheating.
✓ Done when: Scratch project is bug-checked, comments are in, and design spec is updated to match the final version.
Tue Final revision of AI essay — apply feedback from Friday Computer ▾
  1. Open your essay draft in Google Docs.
  2. Look at the two revision goals you wrote on Friday.
  3. Fix those two things first.
  4. Write your conclusion paragraph: restate your thesis in new words, summarize your three reasons briefly, and end with a strong final sentence.
  5. Read the whole essay out loud one more time and fix any sentence that sounds awkward.
💬 "My conclusion starts with: In conclusion, I still believe… My three reasons were… And finally, [strong closing sentence]."
✓ Done when: conclusion is written and the two revision goals from peer feedback are addressed.
Wed Prepare 5-minute showcase: demo the Scratch project + pick essay passages to read Writing ▾
  1. Plan the order of your showcase: will you demo Scratch first or read the essay first?
  2. For the Scratch demo: decide what you'll say while showing it — write 3–4 bullet points as notes.
  3. For the essay: pick the two best sentences from your essay to read aloud.
  4. Practice going through the showcase from start to finish once, talking aloud.
💬 You don't have to memorize anything — having bullet notes is totally fine for a showcase.
✓ Done when: you have a showcase plan and you've run through it once out loud.
Thu Rehearse the full showcase — live Scratch demo practice Discussion ▾
  1. Set up your laptop with Scratch open and your essay on the screen.
  2. Run the full showcase for mom — Scratch demo + essay reading.
  3. Stay under 5 minutes.
  4. After, ask mom: what was the clearest part? What was hardest to follow?
  5. Make one small adjustment based on her feedback.
💬 Treat this exactly like the real showcase — same setup, same talk. It makes the real one much easier.
✓ Done when: full rehearsal completed and one adjustment made based on feedback.
Fri SHOWCASE: Live Scratch demo + persuasive essay reading Showcase ▾
  1. Set up your showcase space with laptop ready.
  2. Demo your Scratch project — explain what it does and show it working.
  3. Read the two best passages from your AI essay.
  4. Tell your audience what you found most interesting or surprising about this unit.
💬 The showcase is a celebration of what you built and argued. You already know this stuff — just show it.
✓ Done when: Scratch demo + essay reading completed for an audience.
Weekly Writing Prompt

Write your essay conclusion: restate your thesis in new words, briefly summarize your three main reasons, and end with a final sentence that's the most memorable in the essay.

"When I think about [AI question], I still believe… The three strongest reasons are… Ultimately, [final sentence that sticks]."

Length: 3–5 sentences. Your last sentence should be the best one.
