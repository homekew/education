Quarter 1 Theme: Animal Biology and Classification

This quarter, we explore the incredible diversity of animal life. We will read the adventure novel Redwall, studying animals through hands-on projects, observation, and creative work. Our big project is an Animal Adaptation Diorama that showcases one creature in its habitat. Through language arts, mathematics, science, and social studies, we will connect ancient civilizations with the animal world they depended on.

Week 1

September 9-12, 2026
Welcome Week
Language Arts
Begin Redwall by Brian Jacques. Set up reading journal with date and chapter summaries. Introduce vocabulary system for new and interesting words encountered in the text.
Math
Pre-assessment and review of fractions. Introduce the concept of ratio using animal examples (e.g., comparing a giraffe's leg length to body height).
Science
What is biology? Explore the Animal Kingdom overview and the five kingdoms of life. Begin thinking about how animals are classified.
Social Studies
What is history? Develop map skills and locate Mesopotamia and Egypt on a world map. Discuss why geography matters.
Thursday Project
Decorate and personalize all notebooks and journals. Create the first entry in your Creature Field Guide. Choose one animal you've observed and make a detailed drawing with notes about what you see.

Week 2

September 15-18, 2026
Mammals
Language Arts
Read Redwall chapters 1-5. Continue reading journal. Brainstorm a real outdoor adventure you've had or witnessed. What happened? What did you see, hear, and feel?
Math
What is a ratio? Explore real-world examples using animal body proportions. Compare the ratio of a cat's tail length to its body length, for example.
Science
Mammal characteristics: fur or hair, warm-blooded, live birth, and milk production. Compare mammals to other animals. Why are we mammals?
Social Studies
Mesopotamia: the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, the first cities, and Sumerian culture. Why did civilizations develop near rivers?
Thursday Project
Add a mammal entry to your Field Guide. Research one mammal (local or exotic) and create an original drawing. Include: name, habitat, diet, one special feature.

Week 3

September 22-25, 2026
Birds and Reptiles
Language Arts
Read Redwall chapters 6-10. Continue reading journal. Begin your personal narrative brainstorm and draft. Describe your outdoor adventure in your own voice.
Math
Equivalent ratios and ratio tables. Compare animal speeds as ratios. Create a table: cheetah, rabbit, human. How many times faster is one than the other?
Science
Bird anatomy: hollow bones, feathers, different types of beaks. Reptile adaptations: scales, ectothermic (cold-blooded) bodies, egg-laying. Observe the differences.
Social Studies
Mesopotamia continued: cuneiform writing, ziggurats, and trade routes. What was the first form of writing? How did it change civilization?
Thursday Project
Find a real feather (or use a reference image) and observe it closely with a magnifying glass if possible. Create a detailed drawing in your Field Guide. Add a bird entry with habitat, diet, and one interesting fact.

Week 4

September 29 - October 2, 2026
Fish and Amphibians
Language Arts
Read Redwall chapters 11-15. Continue reading journal. Complete your personal narrative rough draft. Share with a trusted reader for feedback.
Math
Unit rates: what is a unit rate? Compare animals' speeds, lifespans, and sizes in unit rate form. (Example: miles per hour, years of life, pounds per body length.)
Science
Fish anatomy: gills for breathing underwater, scales, lateral line system for sensing movement. Amphibian metamorphosis: eggs in water, tadpole stage, transformation to adult.
Social Studies
Ancient Egypt: the Nile River and its geography. The role of pharaohs. Why was the Nile so important to Egyptian civilization?
Thursday Project
BEGIN your Animal Adaptation Diorama. Choose your animal. Sketch its habitat on graph paper. List all materials you will need to build this diorama (box, paint, clay, etc.). Research your animal's key adaptations.

Week 5

October 6-9, 2026
Invertebrates
Language Arts
Read Redwall chapters 16-20. Continue reading journal. Revise your personal narrative draft. Look for places to add sensory details and dialogue.
Math
Unit rates continued: miles per hour, price per unit, and real-world problems. Create your own word problems using animal data (e.g., "A hummingbird's heart beats 250 times per minute. How many beats in an hour?").
Science
Insects: 6 legs, 3 body segments, diverse life cycles. Arachnids: spiders and their webs. Worms: segmented bodies and their role in soil. Study how invertebrates compare to vertebrates.
Social Studies
Ancient Egypt: gods and animals. Ra as a falcon, Bastet as a cat, Sobek as a crocodile, Anubis as a jackal. How did Egyptians see animals as divine?
Thursday Project
Begin constructing your diorama. Build the base and paint or create the background habitat scene. Add terrain (clay, moss, fabric, painted cardboard, dried leaves). Create an insect entry in your Field Guide with a detailed drawing.

Week 6

October 13-16, 2026
Food Webs
Language Arts
Finish reading Redwall. Complete reading journal. Prepare for the book quiz by reviewing character names, plot points, and themes.
Math
Fractions review: simplifying fractions, comparing fractions, finding equivalent fractions. Create fraction bars with animal-themed labels for visual understanding.
Science
Food chains vs. food webs. Producers (plants), consumers (herbivores, carnivores, omnivores), and decomposers. Energy pyramids: how much energy transfers at each level?
Social Studies
Ancient Egypt: daily life, farming, and the annual flooding of the Nile. What did Egyptians eat? How did geography shape their food and survival?
Thursday Project
Build a food web mobile (craft project). Draw animals and plants on index cards. Use yarn to connect them showing who eats what. Hang in a window or from a ceiling to display the connections in a food web.

Week 7

October 20-23, 2026
Animal Adaptations Deep Dive
Language Arts
Take the Redwall book quiz. Share your reading journal with a family member. Discuss favorite scenes, characters, and what surprised you about this adventure tale.
Math
Adding and subtracting fractions with unlike denominators. Use animal-themed problems. (Example: A fox eats 1/3 of a meal, a deer eats 2/5 of its meal. How much more did the deer eat?)
Science
Animal Adaptations Deep Dive: camouflage, mimicry, hibernation, migration, venom, and armor. Choose one adaptation to research in depth. How does it help the animal survive?
Social Studies
Ancient Egypt: pyramids, mummification, and hieroglyphics. Complete a hieroglyphics decoder activity. Translate your name or a short message into hieroglyphic symbols.
Thursday Project
Add final touches to your diorama. Place your animal (sculpted from clay, drawn and cut out, or made from felt) in the habitat. Add plants, other animals, food sources. Write a 2-paragraph creature profile: (1) Who is this animal? (2) How is it specifically adapted to survive in its habitat?

Week 8

October 27-30, 2026
Animal Survival Strategies
Language Arts
Begin Wings of Fire (or another fantasy creature novel of your choice). Start a new reading journal with the same format. Note characters, settings, and plot developments as you read.
Math
Multiplying fractions. Use animal examples and real-world contexts. (Example: A recipe calls for 3/4 cup of seeds. If you triple the recipe, how much do you need?)
Science
Predator vs. prey: speed, heightened senses, camouflage, pack hunting strategies, and defense mechanisms. Research one predator and one prey animal. How are they adapted to their roles?
Social Studies
Compare Egypt and Mesopotamia: similarities and differences. Create a graphic organizer (Venn diagram or chart). Begin an illustrated timeline showing key events from both civilizations.
Thursday Project
Diorama presentation rehearsal. Practice presenting your diorama as if leading a nature tour. You should be able to explain: the animal, its biome, three specific adaptations, and one interesting fact. Speak clearly and confidently.

Animal Adaptation Diorama: Project Instructions

1

Choose Your Animal (Week 4 Thursday)

Select an animal that fascinates you. Think about these questions: What biome does it live in? What makes it uniquely adapted to that environment? Is it a mammal, bird, reptile, fish, amphibian, or invertebrate? Sketch the habitat on graph paper and make a list of all the materials you will need to build this diorama.

2

Gather Materials (Week 5 Thursday)

Collect your supplies. Base: a shoebox, small cardboard box, or similar container. Background: draw or paint the habitat scene on cardstock or poster board. Terrain materials: air-dry clay, moss, fabric scraps, painted cardboard pieces, dried leaves, sand, or other natural items. Your animal: you will either sculpt it from clay, draw and cut it out, or make it from felt. Labels and markers for the final touches.

3

Build the Habitat (Week 6 Thursday)

Start with your base and background. Paint or draw the setting that your animal lives in. Consider: Is it a desert, forest, ocean, grassland, or arctic environment? Add terrain to your base. Use clay to sculpt hills or landscapes. Add moss, fabric, or other materials to create texture and depth. The habitat should look realistic and inviting for your animal.

4

Add Your Animal and Details (Week 7 Thursday)

Place your animal in its habitat. Add plants, other animals (if appropriate), and food sources. For example, if your animal is a deer, add grass and maybe a predator in the distance. If it's an owl, add a mouse or other prey. Write a 2-paragraph creature profile: (1) Who is this animal? Describe its physical features, diet, and family structure. (2) How is it adapted to survive? Explain at least 3 specific adaptations and how each helps it thrive.

5

Final Details and Labels (Week 8 Thursday)

Polish your diorama. Add any final touches like extra plants, rocks, or animals. Label key elements of the habitat (e.g., "oak tree," "burrow," "river"). Create an attractive title card with your animal's name. Double-check that everything is secure and the diorama will travel safely if needed.

6

Present Your Diorama (Week 9 Thursday)

Present your finished diorama to your family like a nature guide leading a tour. Explain: (1) What animal is this, and what class does it belong to? (2) Where does it live, and what is that biome like? (3) What are three specific adaptations that help it survive? (4) What is one interesting or surprising fact you learned about this animal? Speak clearly, make eye contact, and enjoy sharing your work.
