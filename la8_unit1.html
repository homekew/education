W1Comics as Literature
Mon Intro to graphic novel genre; read Persepolis ch. 1–3 Reading ▾
  1. Get your copy of Persepolis and open to page 1.
  2. Read chapters 1, 2, and 3. You can read silently or out loud — your choice.
  3. When you finish, close the book and say out loud: "The most surprising thing was…"
  4. Open your journal and write one sentence: "Persepolis is different from a regular comic because…"
💬 Talk option: Skip the journal — tell someone your one observation out loud instead.
✓ Done when: chapters 1–3 read + one observation shared (written or out loud)
Tue Analyze panel layout, speech bubbles, and gutters Reading ▾
  1. Open Persepolis to any page you liked from yesterday.
  2. Point to a gutter (the white space between panels) and say: "The reader has to imagine what happened here."
  3. Find one panel that uses NO words — only pictures. What does the image tell you?
  4. Look at one speech bubble. Does the shape of the bubble change anything?
  5. Write 1–2 sentences in your journal: "I noticed the art shows _____ without using any words."
✓ Done when: one written observation about how art carries meaning
Wed Freewrite: a childhood memory told in 4 panels Writing ▾
  1. Think of one memory from when you were younger — it can be funny, strange, or ordinary.
  2. Say the whole memory out loud like you're telling a friend. (This IS your freewrite.)
  3. Draw 4 boxes in your journal. Rough rectangles are perfect.
  4. Label what goes in each box: Box 1 = beginning, Box 2 = middle, Box 3 = big moment, Box 4 = end.
  5. Add 2–3 words inside each box as a quick label — no full sentences needed.
✓ Done when: 4 labeled boxes sketched in journal
Thu Type up memory narrative; explore Canva comic templates Computer ▾
  1. Open Google Docs and start a new document.
  2. Type a title at the top (like: "The Time I…").
  3. Write 3–5 sentences about your memory. Use your 4-panel sketch as a guide.
  4. Open canva.com in another tab and search "comic strip template."
  5. Open one template you think looks cool and click around to explore it.
  6. Replace one text box in the template with a sentence from your memory.
💬 Sentence starter: "I remember when…" or "One time, something happened that…"
✓ Done when: 3–5 sentences typed in Docs + one Canva template explored
Fri Share memory comics; discuss images vs. words Discussion ▾
  1. Show your 4-panel sketch (and Canva draft if you made one) to someone.
  2. Describe your memory to them in 1–2 sentences.
  3. Discuss together: "What was easier — drawing it out or writing it?"
  4. Answer out loud: "What could pictures do that words couldn't?"
✓ Done when: shared with someone + discussed the question out loud
✍️ Writing Prompt

Think of a moment when you felt out of place or different from everyone else.

Sentence starter: "There was this one time I felt like I didn't belong because…"

Write 3–5 sentences OR draw 2 rough panels that show the moment.

Talk it out first — say the whole thing out loud before you write anything.

W2Character & Dialogue
Mon Persepolis ch. 4–8; how character is built through image and word Reading ▾
  1. Read Persepolis chapters 4–8.
  2. Pick one character besides Marji who stands out to you.
  3. Find a panel where that character's face shows an emotion without any words.
  4. Say out loud: "This character feels _____ because the art shows _____."
  5. Write that sentence in your journal.
✓ Done when: chapters 4–8 read + one character observation written
Tue Dialogue workshop: how characters' voices differ; read March excerpts Reading ▾
  1. Get the March excerpts and read 2–3 pages.
  2. As you read, give each character a different voice (say it out loud if you can).
  3. Notice: do the characters sound different from each other? How?
  4. Say: "Character A sounds _____ because they use _____ kinds of words."
  5. Write one sentence in your journal about each character's voice.
✓ Done when: March pages read + two brief character-voice descriptions written
Wed Write a 2-character dialogue scene — speech only, no action Writing ▾
  1. Think of two characters who disagree about something. (It can be silly — a debate over games, music, food, anything.)
  2. Give them names. Say the names out loud.
  3. Write 6–10 lines of back-and-forth dialogue — only what they say, no action descriptions.
  4. Read it out loud when done. Does it sound like a real argument?
💬 Sentence starters: Character A: "No way, you're wrong because…" / Character B: "That's not true, I think…"
✓ Done when: at least 6 lines of dialogue written and read aloud
Thu Type and format dialogue in Google Docs; practice punctuation rules Computer ▾
  1. Open Google Docs and type your dialogue scene from yesterday.
  2. Rule to remember: each new speaker starts on a new line.
  3. Check every line — does it have quotation marks around what the character says?
  4. Fix any lines that look wrong.
💬 Quick rule: "New speaker = new paragraph."
✓ Done when: dialogue typed and formatted correctly in Google Docs
Fri Act out dialogue scenes; revise what sounds unnatural Kinesthetic ▾
  1. Read your dialogue out loud — you read one character, someone else reads the other (or do both yourself with different voices).
  2. Stop if any line sounds stiff or fake.
  3. Change at least one line so it sounds more natural.
  4. Read the whole thing through one more time.
✓ Done when: read aloud + at least one line revised
✍️ Writing Prompt

Write a scene where two characters disagree about something. Each one should sound different from the other.

Starters: "Character A said, '___.' Character B shot back, '___.' Neither one would back down."

Write 6–8 lines of dialogue. Talk it out first before you write.

Goal: reader can tell who's talking without seeing the names.

✅ Weekly Checklist
W3Visual Description & Setting
Mon Finish Persepolis; analyze how setting creates mood Reading ▾
  1. Finish reading Persepolis.
  2. Pick one setting scene (focus on a place, not a character).
  3. Look at the panels for that location — what shapes, lines, or patterns are used?
  4. Say out loud: "This setting feels _____ because the art uses _____."
  5. Write that sentence in your journal.
✓ Done when: Persepolis finished + one setting observation written
Tue Descriptive writing workshop: sensory language, show don't tell Writing ▾
  1. Pick an object nearby — headphones, a Lego set, a mug, anything.
  2. Look at it for 30 seconds. Don't write yet — just observe.
  3. Write 3 sentences that SHOW the object without naming it. Use senses: what it looks like, feels like, sounds like.
  4. Read your sentences back. Can someone guess what the object is?
💬 Challenge: describe it to someone out loud first, then write it down.
✓ Done when: 3 "show don't tell" sentences written about one object
Wed Write a vivid setting description for your graphic novel world Writing ▾
  1. Think about the world your graphic novel takes place in. You're inventing this — it can be real or imaginary.
  2. Say out loud: "My world looks like _____, smells like _____, and sounds like _____."
  3. Write that sentence down. That's your opening line.
  4. Add 4–6 more sentences — describe what you'd see, hear, smell, and feel if you stepped into this world.
💬 Sentence starter: "If you stepped into my world, the first thing you'd notice is…"
✓ Done when: 5–7 sentences written about your world
Thu Create a world map or environment sketch; add captions in Canva Computer ▾
  1. Draw a rough map or sketch of your world on paper. Label 3–4 important locations.
  2. Open Canva and start a new blank document.
  3. Add your setting description from yesterday as a text block.
  4. Add labels or captions for the most important places in your world.
✓ Done when: paper sketch drawn + setting description placed in Canva with labels
Fri Share settings — can someone draw your world from your words alone? Kinesthetic ▾
  1. Read your setting description out loud to someone — don't show them your sketch yet.
  2. Ask them to draw what they picture as you read.
  3. When they're done, compare their drawing to yours.
  4. Discuss: "What matched? What did my description not explain clearly?"
✓ Done when: setting read aloud + drawings compared and discussed
✍️ Writing Prompt

Describe the world your graphic novel takes place in. Use at least 3 different senses.

Starter: "If you stepped into my world, the first thing you'd notice is… It smells like… and sounds like…"

Write 4–6 sentences. Say it out loud first, then write it down.

Bonus: sketch two panels that show the same place without any words.

✅ Weekly Checklist
W4Plot & Storyboarding
Mon Three-act structure in graphic novels; read Amulet excerpts Reading ▾
  1. Get the Amulet excerpts and read 5–7 pages.
  2. As you read, mentally note: when does the story start, build up, hit a big moment, and end?
  3. After reading, say out loud: "Act 1 is the setup. Act 2 is _____, and Act 3 is _____."
  4. Write those three acts down in your journal as one sentence each.
✓ Done when: Amulet pages read + three-act breakdown written (3 sentences)
Tue Storyboarding techniques; plan your 8-panel chapter beat by beat Writing ▾
  1. Draw 8 boxes on a piece of paper — 2 rows of 4 works great.
  2. In Box 1, write "beginning" and a quick note about what happens first.
  3. Fill in boxes 2–7 — one action or moment per box. Labels and stick figures are perfect.
  4. In Box 8, write "end" and what changes by the final panel.
💬 Say your story out loud from start to finish before you draw anything — it's easier that way.
✓ Done when: 8 boxes filled in with basic labels or sketches
Wed Write the plot outline and major beats for your chapter Writing ▾
  1. Look at your 8-panel storyboard from yesterday.
  2. Write a title for your chapter at the top of a page.
  3. Write 1–2 sentences for each act: what happens in the beginning, middle, and end.
  4. Write one final sentence: "By the end, the main character _____ because _____."
💬 Starter: "My story starts when… Then things get complicated when… By the end…"
✓ Done when: 3-act outline written (4–5 sentences total)
Thu Build rough panel layout in Canva; add placeholder dialogue Computer ▾
  1. Open Canva and search "comic book template."
  2. Pick a layout that has about 6–8 panels.
  3. In each panel, type a few words describing what happens there (use your storyboard as a guide).
  4. Add placeholder dialogue in at least 2 panels — just a rough idea of what the character might say.
✓ Done when: all 8 panels labeled + at least 2 dialogue placeholders added
Fri Share storyboards — does the story flow without words? Discussion ▾
  1. Show your Canva storyboard to someone without explaining it first.
  2. Ask them: "Without me telling you — what do you think happens in this story?"
  3. Listen carefully. Note what they understood and what was confusing.
  4. Say out loud: "I need to make panel _____ clearer because _____."
✓ Done when: storyboard shared + one clarity improvement identified
✍️ Writing Prompt

Write a quick pitch for your graphic novel chapter — like you're telling a friend what it's about in 30 seconds.

Starters: "My story is about… The big moment is when… By the end…"

Write 3–4 sentences. Talk it out first, then write it.

Bonus: write one sentence about what changes between panel 1 and panel 8.

✅ Weekly Checklist
W5Drafting the Chapter
Mon Begin full digital draft: panels 1–4 in Canva Computer ▾
  1. Open your Canva storyboard from last week.
  2. Work on panels 1–4: replace the placeholder labels with real dialogue or narration text.
  3. Make sure each panel has at least one line of text (dialogue or a narration box).
  4. Don't worry about perfection — getting words in the panels is the goal today.
✓ Done when: panels 1–4 have real dialogue or narration text placed
Tue Continue drafting panels 5–8; add narration and inner monologue Computer ▾
  1. Continue in Canva, working on panels 5–8.
  2. Add at least one narration box somewhere in your chapter — text that shows what the character is thinking, not saying.
  3. Make sure all 8 panels now have something in them.
💬 Narration starters: "Meanwhile…" / "She didn't know that…" / "All I could think was…"
✓ Done when: all 8 panels have text placed + at least one narration box added
Wed Write artist's statement — why you made the choices you did Writing ▾
  1. Open Google Docs and make a new document called "Artist's Statement."
  2. Answer this question in 1–2 sentences: "Why did I pick this story?"
  3. Answer this question: "What did I want the reader to feel?"
  4. Answer this question: "What is one choice I made on purpose?" (like a font, color, panel size, or image angle)
  5. Put it all together — aim for 5–8 sentences total.
💬 Starters: "I chose this story because…" / "I wanted the reader to feel…" / "One choice I made was… because…"
✓ Done when: artist's statement written (at least 5 sentences, 3 questions answered)
Thu Peer workshop: get feedback using a structured rubric Discussion ▾
  1. Share your Canva chapter with someone (show it on screen or print it out).
  2. Ask them two specific questions: "Does the story make sense?" and "Is there anything confusing?"
  3. Listen to their feedback without arguing — just take notes.
  4. Write down one specific change you want to make based on what you heard.
✓ Done when: feedback received + one change written down
Fri Revise panels based on feedback; refine digital layout Computer ▾
  1. Look at the change you wrote down yesterday.
  2. Open Canva and make that change.
  3. Flip through all 8 panels in order — does anything feel out of place or hard to follow?
  4. Fix one more thing that bothers you.
✓ Done when: at least two revisions made in Canva
✍️ Writing Prompt

Write your artist's statement. Answer 3 questions: Why this story? What did you want the reader to feel? What one visual choice did you make on purpose?

Starters: "I chose this story because…" / "I wanted the reader to feel…" / "One choice I made was…"

5–8 sentences total. Say all 3 answers out loud before you write anything.

You do NOT need to write 300 words — 5 strong sentences is excellent.

W6Polish & Showcase Prep
Mon Final revision of all 8 panels — clean up and align Computer ▾
  1. Open your Canva chapter.
  2. Go through each panel one at a time — check: Is the text readable? Does it look clean?
  3. Fix any overlapping text boxes or anything that looks messy.
  4. Make sure all 8 panels are in the right order.
✓ Done when: all 8 panels reviewed and cleaned up
Tue Export chapter as PDF; do a final proofread of all text Computer ▾
  1. In Canva, click "Share" then "Download" and choose PDF.
  2. Open the PDF and read every word carefully.
  3. Circle or note any spelling mistakes or weird sentences.
  4. Go back into Canva and fix those mistakes.
  5. Export the PDF one more time with the fixes saved.
✓ Done when: chapter exported as PDF + spelling errors fixed
Wed Prepare your 3-minute showcase presentation Writing ▾
  1. Open Google Docs and write answers to these 3 questions:
  2. "What is my graphic novel chapter about?" (2 sentences)
  3. "What was the hardest part to create?" (1–2 sentences)
  4. "What am I most proud of?" (1 sentence)
  5. Practice saying all 3 answers out loud once through.
💬 You only need these notes — you don't need to memorize anything. You can look at the page when you present.
✓ Done when: 3 questions answered + practiced out loud once
Thu Practice presenting; point to specific panels while speaking Kinesthetic ▾
  1. Open your chapter on screen or hold the printed version.
  2. Give your full presentation to someone (use the notes from yesterday).
  3. Point to at least 2 specific panels while you talk.
  4. Ask your listener: "Was there anything confusing or hard to follow?"
✓ Done when: full practice run completed with someone listening
Fri SHOWCASE: Present your graphic novel chapter Showcase ▾
  1. Take a few slow breaths. You've done all the work — you're ready.
  2. Open your chapter on screen (or bring the printed version).
  3. Give your presentation using the notes you practiced.
  4. Point to at least 2 specific panels while you speak.
  5. Answer any questions from your audience. You know this story better than anyone.
✓ Done when: presentation complete — you did it! 🎉
✍️ Writing Prompt

Write a short reflection on the whole unit. Three questions to answer:

Starters: "The hardest part was…" / "I surprised myself when…" / "If I made a second chapter, it would be about…"

3–5 sentences is perfect. Say it out loud first, then write it down.
