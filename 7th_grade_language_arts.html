At a Glance

This curriculum is designed for a 7th grade student who loves fantasy novels with animal characters, hands-on art projects, nature study, and creative making. Every unit weaves her passions into rigorous, standards-aligned work.

📅Duration36 weeks · 6 units of 6 weeks
🌲StandardsOregon ELA (CCSS-aligned)
🎨ApproachProject-based & flexible
📖Text TypesFantasy novels, nature essays, informational articles, graphic novels
🖼️Art ProjectsDioramas, nature journals, world atlases, clay figures, posters, portfolio
⭐Final ProductYear-end portfolio with original writing, art & reflection

The Six Units

Each unit is 6 weeks long and includes a novel or text study, daily writing practice, grammar mini-lessons, and a hands-on signature project that integrates art with literacy standards.

1

Into the Wild

Animal Fantasy Novel Study
Weeks 1–6

"Who are we through the eyes of an animal? Dive into a world where creatures lead, choose, and dream."

Students launch the year with a deep dive into an animal fantasy novel of their choosing. Through close reading and imaginative projects, they practice citing evidence, analyzing character and theme, and writing their own narrative — all grounded in the natural world.

📚 Suggested Texts
  • Mrs. Frisby and the Rats of NIMH — Robert C. O'Brien (mice & rats; award-winning, rich for analysis)
  • Watership Down — Richard Adams (rabbits; adapted chapters welcome)
  • Silverwing — Kenneth Oppel (bats; gripping adventure, great world-building)
  • Tailchaser's Song — Tad Williams (cats; epic fantasy, good Warriors follow-up)
  • Varjak Paw — SF Said (young Mesopotamian cat on a hero's journey; beautiful writing, great for Warriors fans)

Students build a 3D diorama of a significant setting from their novel using clay, paint, natural materials, and recycled items. They write a 1-page scene card identifying the setting, its importance to the plot, and a cited quote from the text. Displayed for a gallery walk in Week 6.

Nature's Voice

Informational Reading & Nature Writing
Weeks 7–12

"The natural world has a story to tell. Scientists, writers, and artists all help us hear it."

Students shift to nonfiction, reading compelling essays and articles about animals and ecosystems. They practice identifying central ideas, evaluating arguments, and gathering research — then channel their findings into a beautiful illustrated nature journal.

📚 Suggested Texts
  • Essays by Annie Dillard, Aldo Leopold, or Robin Wall Kimmerer (excerpts)
  • Articles from National Geographic, Smithsonian, or Oregon Dept. of Fish & Wildlife
  • An Oregon wildlife fact sheet or species profile (local connection!)
  • Short informational texts on ecosystems, animal behavior, or conservation

The Hero's Journey

Fantasy Novel Study & Argumentative Writing
Weeks 13–18

"Every hero must choose. What shapes a character — and what do those choices reveal about our world?"

Students read a longer fantasy novel, mapping the classic hero's journey while analyzing character development, point of view, and author's craft. They write a substantive argumentative essay and create a hand-illustrated world atlas as their signature project.

📚 Suggested Texts
  • Ratha's Creature — Clare Bell (prehistoric wild cat society; female protagonist; one of the best animal fantasy novels ever written)
  • The Call of the Wild — Jack London
  • My Side of the Mountain — Jean Craighead George
  • Julie of the Wolves — Jean Craighead George
  • The Jungle Book — Rudyard Kipling (selected stories)

Speak Up for the Wild

Research, Argument & Environmental Advocacy
Weeks 25–30

"The animals can't speak for themselves. Who will? Research is how you find your argument. Your voice is how you use it."

Students choose a real wildlife or environmental issue they care about — endangered species, habitat loss, ocean health, Oregon's old-growth forests — and research it deeply. They read multiple sources, evaluate arguments, and write a compelling advocacy letter or essay, culminating in a visual presentation.

📚 Suggested Texts
  • Current articles from Oregon Wild, National Wildlife Federation, Oregon Dept. of Fish & Wildlife
  • Excerpts from Braiding Sweetgrass — Robin Wall Kimmerer (accessible chapters)
  • Opinion pieces and counterarguments on chosen environmental topic
  • Primary sources: species fact sheets, environmental impact summaries

My Story, My Voice

Independent Reading, Original Novel Opening & Portfolio
Weeks 31–36

"This is your year. What did you learn? What do you have to say? Now write it."

The final unit is student-driven. Each student selects a fantasy novel of their choice, conducts an independent literary analysis, and writes the opening of their own original novel. The unit culminates in a portfolio collecting the year's best work and a cover art piece representing the author they've become.

📚 Suggested Texts (Student Choice)
  • Any fantasy novel featuring animals or nature — student's free choice!
  • Howl's Moving Castle — Diana Wynne Jones
  • The Sight — David Clement-Davies (wolves; mystical, lyrical, sweeping epic)
  • Wild Magic — Tamora Pierce (animal communication; female protagonist)
  • The Girl Who Drank the Moon — Kelly Barnhill

Grammar & Language Scope and Sequence

Grammar is taught in short daily or weekly mini-lessons woven into the writing process. Each grammar concept connects directly to the unit's writing focus.

UnitGrammar & Language Focus
Unit 1 (Wks 1–6)Phrases vs. clauses · Noun & verb phrases · Prepositional & participial phrases · Complex sentences · Subject-verb agreement
Unit 2 (Wks 7–12)Sentence variety (simple/compound/complex) · Coordination & subordination · Conjunctive adverbs · Transitions · Denotation vs. connotation · Precise word choice
Unit 3 (Wks 13–18)Adjective & adverb clauses · Misplaced & dangling modifiers · Active vs. passive voice · Comma rules · Complex sentence editing
Unit 4 (Wks 19–24)Figurative language (simile, metaphor, personification, symbolism) · Connotation vs. denotation · Punctuating dialogue · Direct vs. indirect speech · Vivid verbs
Unit 5 (Wks 25–30)Formal vs. informal register · Domain-specific vocabulary · Hedging language · Transitions in argument · Sentence-level precision
Unit 6 (Wks 31–36)Comprehensive review of all prior concepts · Consistent verb tense · Point of view consistency · Proofreading strategies · Spelling review

Notes for the Home Educator

Practical guidance for making this curriculum sing.

🌊 Flexibility First

This curriculum is designed to flex around your daughter's energy and pace. If a unit sparks deep enthusiasm (and it will — she'll probably spend three weeks on the diorama), let it breathe. Compress a later unit if needed. Standards will be met across the arc of the year, not week by week.

🎨 Art Is Core Learning

The hands-on projects are not "extras" — they are central. Building a diorama deepens setting analysis. Painting a world map builds understanding of place. Sculpting a character from clay is character analysis in three dimensions. Celebrate the making.

💬 Discussion Over Testing

For a project-based learner, a rich 20-minute conversation about the book often tells you more than a written test. Oral discussion is itself a standard (SL.7.1). Use the discussion prompts and follow her curiosity — that's assessment too.

✏️ Writing as a Process

The W.7.5 standard emphasizes process over product — planning, drafting, revising, editing. Don't rush to a polished final draft. Let messy first drafts be messy. The revision conversations are where the real learning happens.

🌲 Oregon Connection

Wherever possible, connect informational units to Oregon's specific ecosystems — the Cascade Range, old-growth forests, the high desert, Pacific salmon, wolves, osprey. This makes the research personal and the advocacy real. Oregon Wild is a great local resource.

📚 Student Choice Matters

The suggested books are starting points. If she's deep into the Warriors series and wants to use book 4 instead of book 1, let her. If she wants to build her diorama out of polymer clay instead of air-dry clay, that's great too. Ownership of her learning is the goal.
